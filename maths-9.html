Chapter 1: Number Systems

1.1 Introduction to Numbers
Numbers are the foundation of mathematics. The set of natural numbers N = {1,2,3,...}. Whole numbers W = {0,1,2,...}. Integers Z = {...,-3,-2,-1,0,1,2,3,...}. Rational numbers Q = {p/q | p,q ∈ Z, q≠0}. Rational numbers include terminating decimals (0.5) and non-terminating repeating decimals (0.333...). Irrational numbers are numbers that cannot be expressed as p/q; their decimal expansions are non-terminating and non-repeating. Examples: √2, √3, π, e.

1.2 Real Numbers
The union of rational and irrational numbers is the set of real numbers R. Every real number corresponds to a unique point on the number line, and vice versa (the number line is complete). Real numbers are closed under addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division (except by zero).

1.3 Representing Irrational Numbers on the Number Line
√2 can be located by constructing a right triangle with legs 1 and 1. The hypotenuse is √2. Using a compass, transfer this length to the number line. Similarly, √3 can be constructed using a triangle with legs 1 and √2. This spiral method allows us to locate any √n geometrically. For example, √5 = √(2²+1²).

1.4 Operations on Real Numbers
Real numbers satisfy the same laws of operations as rational numbers: closure, commutativity, associativity, distributivity. The sum, difference, product, and quotient (denominator non-zero) of two real numbers is a real number. However, adding a rational and an irrational gives an irrational number. For example, 2 + √3 is irrational. Multiplying a non-zero rational by an irrational gives an irrational number. Example: 5√2 is irrational.

1.5 Square Roots and Rationalisation
For positive real numbers, √(ab) = √a × √b and √(a/b) = √a/√b. Rationalisation is the process of eliminating irrational numbers from the denominator. Example: 1/√2 = √2/2. To rationalise 1/(√a + √b), multiply numerator and denominator by (√a - √b). This uses the identity (a+b)(a-b)=a²-b². Example: 1/(√5+√3) = (√5-√3)/(5-3) = (√5-√3)/2.

1.6 Laws of Exponents for Real Numbers
For a > 0 and real numbers m,n:
a^m × a^n = a^(m+n)
(a^m)^n = a^(mn)
a^m / a^n = a^(m-n)
a^0 = 1
a^(-m) = 1/a^m
(ab)^m = a^m b^m
(a/b)^m = a^m / b^m
a^(m/n) = (a^(1/n))^m = nth root of a^m.

1.7 Numerical Examples
1. Simplify √50 + √18 = 5√2 + 3√2 = 8√2.
2. Rationalise 1/(√5 - √3) = (√5+√3)/(5-3) = (√5+√3)/2.
3. Simplify (2√3 + 3√2)(2√3 - 3√2) = (2√3)² - (3√2)² = 12 - 18 = -6.
4. Find the value of (81)^(3/4) = (81^(1/4))³ = 3³ = 27.

📐 √(ab) = √a × √b | a^(m/n) = ⁿ√(a^m)

Chapter 2: Polynomials

2.1 Definition and Types
A polynomial is an algebraic expression of the form p(x)=a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1}+...+a_1 x + a_0, where a_n ≠ 0 and exponents are non-negative integers. The highest exponent n is the degree. Types: linear (degree 1), quadratic (degree 2), cubic (degree 3), biquadratic (degree 4), etc. Constant polynomial has degree 0. Zero polynomial has no degree defined.

2.2 Zeros of a Polynomial
A zero (or root) of a polynomial p(x) is a number c such that p(c)=0. For a linear polynomial ax+b, zero = -b/a. Geometrically, zeros are the x-intercepts of the graph. A linear polynomial has exactly one zero. A quadratic polynomial can have at most two zeros. A cubic polynomial can have at most three zeros. The number of zeros is at most the degree.

2.3 Quadratic Polynomials and the Quadratic Formula
For a quadratic polynomial ax²+bx+c, the zeros are given by x = [-b ± √(b²-4ac)] / (2a). The discriminant D = b²-4ac determines the nature: D>0 (two distinct real zeros), D=0 (one repeated real zero), D<0 (no real zeros, but two complex zeros).

2.4 Relationship between Zeros and Coefficients
For quadratic polynomial x² + Sx + P = 0, sum of zeros = -b/a = S, product = c/a = P. For cubic ax³+bx²+cx+d, sum of zeros = -b/a, sum of product of zeros taken two at a time = c/a, product = -d/a.

2.5 Remainder and Factor Theorems
Remainder theorem: If p(x) is divided by (x-a), the remainder is p(a). Factor theorem: (x-a) is a factor of p(x) if and only if p(a)=0. These are used for factorisation and finding zeros.

2.6 Algebraic Identities
(x+y)² = x²+2xy+y²; (x-y)² = x²-2xy+y²; x²-y² = (x-y)(x+y); (x+y+z)² = x²+y²+z²+2xy+2yz+2zx; (x+y)³ = x³+y³+3xy(x+y); (x-y)³ = x³-y³-3xy(x-y); x³+y³ = (x+y)(x²-xy+y²); x³-y³ = (x-y)(x²+xy+y²).

2.7 Factorisation of Polynomials
Methods: common factor, regrouping, using identities, splitting the middle term (for quadratics), factor theorem. Example: x²+5x+6 = (x+2)(x+3).

2.8 Numerical Examples
1. Find zeros of p(x)=x²-5x+6. Factor: (x-2)(x-3)=0 ⇒ x=2,3.
2. Using remainder theorem, find remainder when x³-2x²+3x-4 is divided by (x-2). p(2)=8-8+6-4=2.
3. If α and β are zeros of x²-3x+2, find α+β and αβ. α+β=3, αβ=2.

📐 (a+b)² = a²+2ab+b² | a²-b²=(a-b)(a+b) | Remainder = p(a)

Chapter 3: Coordinate Geometry

3.1 Cartesian Plane
The Cartesian plane is formed by two perpendicular number lines: the x-axis (horizontal) and y-axis (vertical). Their intersection is the origin O(0,0). Any point P is represented by an ordered pair (x,y) – abscissa (x-coordinate) and ordinate (y-coordinate). This system was introduced by René Descartes.

3.2 Quadrants
The axes divide the plane into four quadrants: Quadrant I: x>0,y>0; II: x<0,y>0; III: x<0,y<0; IV: x>0,y<0. Points on axes have either x=0 (y-axis) or y=0 (x-axis). The origin has both coordinates zero.

3.3 Plotting Points
To plot a point (x,y), start at the origin. Move x units horizontally (right if positive, left if negative). Then move y units vertically (up if positive, down if negative). Mark the point.

3.4 Distance Formula
The distance between points A(x₁,y₁) and B(x₂,y₂) is AB = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]. This is derived from the Pythagorean theorem. Example: Distance between (2,3) and (5,7) = √[(5-2)²+(7-3)²] = √(9+16)=√25=5.

3.5 Section Formula and Midpoint
If a point P divides AB internally in ratio m:n, then P = ((mx₂+nx₁)/(m+n), (my₂+ny₁)/(m+n)). Midpoint (m=n=1): ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2). Example: Midpoint of (2,3) and (4,7) is (3,5).

3.6 Area of Triangle
Area = ½ |x₁(y₂-y₃) + x₂(y₃-y₁) + x₃(y₁-y₂)|. If area = 0, points are collinear. Example: For (0,0), (4,0), (0,3), area = ½ |0(0-3)+4(3-0)+0(0-0)| = ½×12 = 6 sq units.

3.7 Applications
Coordinate geometry is used in navigation, computer graphics, physics (motion), and engineering.

📐 Distance = √[(x₂-x₁)²+(y₂-y₁)²] | Midpoint = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)

Chapter 4: Linear Equations in Two Variables

4.1 Linear Equation – Standard Form
A linear equation in two variables is of the form ax + by + c = 0, where a, b, c are real numbers, and a and b are not both zero. The graph of such an equation is a straight line. Example: 2x + 3y = 6 can be written as 2x + 3y - 6 = 0. Every solution (x,y) that satisfies the equation corresponds to a point on the line, and every point on the line is a solution.

4.2 Solution of a Linear Equation
A linear equation in two variables has infinitely many solutions. For each value of x, we can find a corresponding y. Example: For 2x+3y=6, if x=0, then y=2; if x=3, then y=0; if x=-3, then y=4, etc. These solutions can be plotted to draw the line.

4.3 Graph of a Linear Equation
To graph a linear equation: (i) Find at least two solutions (e.g., x-intercept where y=0, y-intercept where x=0). (ii) Plot these points on the Cartesian plane. (iii) Join them with a straight line. The line extends infinitely in both directions.

4.4 Slope-Intercept Form
Any linear equation can be rewritten as y = mx + c, where m is the slope (gradient) and c is the y-intercept. Slope m = (y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁) = tanθ, where θ is the angle the line makes with the positive x-axis. Parallel lines have equal slopes; perpendicular lines have slopes that multiply to -1.

4.5 System of Linear Equations
Two linear equations in two variables can have: (i) a unique solution (intersecting lines, different slopes), (ii) no solution (parallel lines, same slope but different intercepts), (iii) infinitely many solutions (coincident lines, same slope and intercept). Solving methods: substitution, elimination, cross-multiplication.

4.6 Applications
Linear equations model real-world situations: cost and revenue, speed-time-distance, age problems, mixture problems. Example: The sum of two numbers is 10 and their difference is 4. Let numbers be x and y. x+y=10, x-y=4 ⇒ adding: 2x=14 ⇒ x=7, y=3.

4.7 Numerical Examples
1. Find the slope of the line passing through (2,3) and (5,11). m = (11-3)/(5-2)=8/3 ≈ 2.67.
2. Write 3x-4y=12 in slope-intercept form. -4y = -3x+12 ⇒ y = (3/4)x - 3. Slope = 3/4, y-intercept = -3.
3. Solve by elimination: 2x+y=8, x-y=1. Adding: 3x=9 ⇒ x=3, then y=2.

📐 y = mx + c | Slope m = (y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁) | Ax + By + C = 0

Chapter 5: Introduction to Euclid's Geometry

5.1 Euclid and the Elements
Euclid, a Greek mathematician (around 300 BCE), wrote the book "Elements", which became the foundation of geometry for over 2000 years. He introduced the axiomatic method: starting with definitions, axioms (common notions), and postulates (geometric assumptions), then proving theorems logically.

5.2 Euclid's Definitions
Some definitions: A point has no part. A line has length without breadth. A straight line is a line that lies evenly with points on itself. A surface has length and breadth only. A plane surface is a surface that lies evenly with straight lines on itself. A circle is a plane figure bounded by a line such that all distances from a fixed point (center) are equal.

5.3 Euclid's Axioms (Common Notions)
Things equal to the same thing are equal to one another. If equals are added to equals, the wholes are equal. If equals are subtracted from equals, the remainders are equal. Things that coincide with one another are equal. The whole is greater than the part. Things that are double of the same thing are equal. Things that are halves of the same thing are equal.

5.4 Euclid's Postulates
1. A straight line can be drawn from any point to any other point.
2. A finite straight line can be extended continuously in a straight line.
3. A circle can be drawn with any center and any radius.
4. All right angles are equal to one another.
5. If a straight line falling on two straight lines makes the interior angles on the same side less than two right angles, then the two lines, if extended indefinitely, meet on that side. This is the parallel postulate, equivalent to: Through a point not on a line, exactly one parallel line can be drawn.

5.5 Importance of Axioms and Postulates
They are self-evident truths that do not require proof. All theorems are derived from them. Non-Euclidean geometries exist where the fifth postulate is changed (e.g., spherical geometry).

5.6 Example of a Theorem
Theorem: If a line segment is extended, the whole is greater than the part. This follows from axiom 5.

📐 Euclid's fifth postulate: Through a point not on a line, exactly one parallel line exists.

Chapter 6: Lines and Angles

6.1 Basic Terms
A line segment has two endpoints. A ray has one endpoint and extends infinitely in one direction. An angle is formed when two rays originate from a common endpoint (vertex). Angles are measured in degrees (°). Types: acute (<90°), right (90°), obtuse (90°-180°), straight (180°), reflex (180°-360°), full (360°). Complementary angles sum to 90°. Supplementary angles sum to 180°.

6.2 Intersecting Lines and Vertically Opposite Angles
When two lines intersect, they form two pairs of vertically opposite angles, which are equal. Adjacent angles on a straight line are supplementary (linear pair axiom).

6.3 Parallel Lines and a Transversal
A transversal is a line that intersects two or more lines at distinct points. If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then: corresponding angles are equal, alternate interior angles are equal, and consecutive interior angles (co-interior) are supplementary. Conversely, if any of these conditions hold, the lines are parallel.

6.4 Angle Sum Property of a Triangle
The sum of the three interior angles of a triangle is 180°. This is a fundamental property. Proof: Draw a line parallel to one side through the opposite vertex, then use alternate interior angles. An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two opposite interior angles (remote interior angles).

6.5 Numerical Examples
1. In a triangle, two angles are 45° and 65°. Find the third angle. 180 - (45+65) = 70°.
2. If a transversal cuts two parallel lines and one interior angle is 120°, find the consecutive interior angle. Supplementary ⇒ 60°.
3. Find the exterior angle of a triangle with interior opposite angles 40° and 60°. Exterior = 100°.

📐 Sum of angles in triangle = 180° | Exterior angle = sum of remote interior angles

Chapter 7: Triangles

7.1 Classification of Triangles
By sides: equilateral (all sides equal), isosceles (two sides equal), scalene (all sides different). By angles: acute (all angles <90°), right (one angle 90°), obtuse (one angle >90°).

7.2 Congruence of Triangles
Two triangles are congruent if all corresponding sides and angles are equal. Criteria: SSS (Side-Side-Side), SAS (Side-Angle-Side), ASA (Angle-Side-Angle), AAS (Angle-Angle-Side), RHS (Right angle-Hypotenuse-Side) for right triangles.

7.3 Properties of Triangles
In an isosceles triangle, angles opposite equal sides are equal. In a triangle, the sum of any two sides is greater than the third. The angle opposite the longer side is larger, and vice versa.

7.4 Inequalities in Triangles
For any triangle, the sum of any two sides > third side. This is used to check if a triangle is possible. Also, the side opposite the greater angle is longer.

7.5 Numerical Examples
1. Check if sides 3 cm, 4 cm, 5 cm form a triangle. 3+4>5, 4+5>3, 5+3>4 ⇒ Yes. Also, 3²+4²=5² ⇒ right triangle.
2. In an isosceles triangle with base angles 45°, find the vertex angle. Vertex = 180-2×45 = 90°.
3. In triangle ABC, if AB=AC and ∠B=70°, find ∠A. ∠C=70°, ∠A=180-140=40°.

📐 SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, RHS congruence rules. Sum of two sides > third side.

Chapter 8: Quadrilaterals

8.1 Definition and Angle Sum
A quadrilateral is a polygon with four sides, four vertices, and four angles. The sum of interior angles is 360°. Types: parallelogram, rectangle, square, rhombus, trapezium, kite.

8.2 Properties of Parallelogram
Opposite sides are parallel and equal. Opposite angles are equal. Diagonals bisect each other. Adjacent angles are supplementary.

8.3 Special Parallelograms
Rectangle: All angles 90°, diagonals equal. Rhombus: All sides equal, diagonals perpendicular bisectors, diagonals bisect angles. Square: All properties of rectangle and rhombus.

8.4 Trapezium and Kite
Trapezium: One pair of opposite sides parallel. Kite: Two pairs of adjacent sides equal; one diagonal bisects the other at right angles.

8.5 Midpoint Theorem
The line segment joining the midpoints of two sides of a triangle is parallel to the third side and half its length. Converse: A line through the midpoint of one side parallel to another side bisects the third side.

8.6 Numerical Examples
1. In a parallelogram, one angle is 70°. Find all angles. Opposite angles equal: 70°, 110°, 70°, 110°.
2. In a rhombus, diagonals are 6 cm and 8 cm. Find side. Side = √((6/2)²+(8/2)²)=√(9+16)=5 cm.
3. In a rectangle, if length is 10 cm and breadth 6 cm, find diagonal. Diagonal = √(10²+6²)=√136≈11.66 cm.

📐 Sum of interior angles = 360° | Midpoint theorem: DE ∥ BC and DE = ½ BC

Chapter 9: Circles

9.1 Basic Definitions
A circle is the set of all points equidistant from a fixed point (center). Radius (r) = distance from center to any point. Diameter = 2r. Chord is a line segment joining two points on the circle. Secant is a line that intersects the circle at two points. Tangent touches at one point. Arc is a part of the circumference. Sector is region between radii and arc. Segment is region between chord and arc.

9.2 Angle at the Center and Circumference
The angle subtended by an arc at the center is twice the angle subtended at any point on the remaining part of the circle. Angles in the same segment are equal. The angle in a semicircle is a right angle (90°).

9.3 Chords and Distances
Equal chords subtend equal angles at the center. The perpendicular from the center to a chord bisects the chord. Equal chords are equidistant from the center, and conversely.

9.4 Cyclic Quadrilaterals
A quadrilateral is cyclic if its vertices lie on a circle. The sum of opposite angles is 180°. Conversely, if the sum of opposite angles is 180°, the quadrilateral is cyclic.

9.5 Numerical Examples
1. A chord of length 8 cm is at a distance of 3 cm from the center. Find radius. r = √(3²+4²)=5 cm.
2. In a cyclic quadrilateral, one angle is 70°, find its opposite angle. 180-70=110°.
3. The angle subtended by an arc at the center is 100°. Find the angle at the circumference. = 50°.

📐 Angle at center = 2 × angle at circumference | Opposite angles of cyclic quadrilateral sum to 180°

Chapter 10: Heron's Formula

10.1 Need for Heron's Formula
To find the area of a triangle when all three sides are known, but height is not given, Heron's formula provides a direct method. It is especially useful for scalene triangles and for finding the area of quadrilaterals by dividing them into triangles.

10.2 Heron's Formula
For a triangle with sides a, b, c, the semi-perimeter s = (a+b+c)/2. Then area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)].

10.3 Application to Quadrilaterals
To find the area of a quadrilateral, split it into two triangles along a diagonal, calculate the area of each triangle using Heron's formula, and add them.

10.4 Numerical Examples
1. Find area of triangle with sides 3,4,5. s = (3+4+5)/2 = 6. Area = √[6×3×2×1] = √36 = 6 sq units.
2. A triangle has sides 13,14,15 cm. s=21. Area = √[21×8×7×6] = √7056 = 84 cm².
3. Find area of an equilateral triangle of side 10 cm. s=15, area = √[15×5×5×5] = √1875 = 25√3 ≈ 43.3 cm².

📐 s = (a+b+c)/2 | Area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]

Chapter 11: Surface Areas and Volumes

11.1 Cuboid and Cube
Cuboid: Length l, breadth b, height h. Lateral Surface Area (LSA) = 2h(l+b). Total Surface Area (TSA) = 2(lb+bh+hl). Volume = l×b×h.
Cube: side a. LSA = 4a², TSA = 6a², Volume = a³.

11.2 Cylinder
Right circular cylinder: radius r, height h. Curved Surface Area (CSA) = 2πrh. TSA = 2πr(r+h). Volume = πr²h.

11.3 Cone
Radius r, height h, slant height l = √(r²+h²). CSA = πrl. TSA = πr(r+l). Volume = (1/3)πr²h.

11.4 Sphere and Hemisphere
Sphere: Surface Area = 4πr², Volume = (4/3)πr³.
Hemisphere: CSA = 2πr², TSA = 3πr², Volume = (2/3)πr³.

11.5 Numerical Examples
1. Volume of cuboid 10×5×4 = 200 cm³.
2. Curved surface area of cylinder r=7, h=10: CSA = 2×22/7×7×10 = 440 cm².
3. Volume of sphere r=3: (4/3)π×27 = 36π ≈ 113.1 cm³.
4. Slant height of cone with r=3, h=4: l=5 cm, CSA=π×3×5=15π≈47.1 cm².

📐 Cuboid: V = l×b×h | Cylinder: V = πr²h | Cone: V = ⅓πr²h | Sphere: V = ⁴⁄₃πr³

Chapter 12: Statistics

12.1 Data and its Types
Data are facts or figures collected for analysis. Raw data is unorganized. Primary data is collected directly; secondary data is from other sources. Data can be ungrouped (individual values) or grouped (class intervals).

12.2 Measures of Central Tendency
Mean (Average): Sum of all observations / number of observations. Median: Middle value when data arranged in order. For odd n, median = middle; for even n, median = average of two middle values. Mode: The value that appears most frequently. A data set can have one mode, multiple modes, or no mode.

12.3 Graphical Representation
Bar graph: Rectangular bars with heights proportional to frequencies. Histogram: Bar graph without gaps for grouped continuous data. Frequency polygon: Line graph joining midpoints of histogram bars.

12.4 Range and Variability
Range = maximum value - minimum value. It gives a rough measure of spread.

12.5 Numerical Examples
1. Find mean of 2,4,6,8,10: (2+4+6+8+10)/5 = 30/5 = 6.
2. Find median of 3,5,7,9,11: middle is 7.
3. Find mode of 1,2,2,3,4: mode = 2.
4. Find range of 5,15,25,35: range = 35-5=30.

📐 Mean = Σx/n | Median = middle value | Mode = most frequent

Chapter 13: Probability

13.1 Introduction to Probability
Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring. It is a number between 0 and 1 inclusive. 0 means impossible, 1 means certain. For equally likely outcomes, P(E) = Number of favourable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes.

13.2 Experiments and Outcomes
An experiment is a procedure that yields a result. The set of all possible outcomes is the sample space. Example: Tossing a coin → sample space {H, T}, P(H)=1/2. Rolling a die → sample space {1,2,3,4,5,6}, P(6)=1/6, P(even)=3/6=1/2.

13.3 Complementary Events
The probability of an event not happening is P(not E) = 1 - P(E). Example: P(head)=0.5, P(not head)=0.5.

13.4 Probability in Daily Life
Weather forecasts, games of chance, insurance, quality control.

13.5 Numerical Examples
1. What is the probability of drawing an ace from a deck of 52 cards? 4/52 = 1/13 ≈ 0.0769.
2. Two coins are tossed. Probability of at least one head? Possible outcomes: HH, HT, TH, TT → 3 favourable → 3/4 = 0.75.
3. A die is rolled. Probability of getting a number >4? Favourable: 5,6 → 2/6=1/3.

📐 P(E) = favourable/total | 0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1 | P(not E) = 1 - P(E)
