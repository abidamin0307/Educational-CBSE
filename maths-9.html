Chapter 1: Number Systems

Number systems form the foundation of mathematics. The natural numbers (N) are counting numbers: 1, 2, 3, ... Whole numbers (W) include zero: 0, 1, 2, ... Integers (Z) include negative numbers: ..., -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, ... Rational numbers (Q) are numbers that can be expressed as p/q where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0. Examples: 2/3, -5/7, 4, 0. Rational numbers include terminating decimals (0.5 = 1/2) and non-terminating repeating decimals (0.333... = 1/3).

Irrational numbers cannot be expressed as p/q. Their decimal expansions are non-terminating and non-repeating. Examples: √2, √3, π, e. The set of real numbers (R) is the union of rational and irrational numbers. Every real number corresponds to a unique point on the number line, and vice versa.

Operations on real numbers follow the same laws as rational numbers. The square root of a positive real number is defined. Rationalisation is the process of eliminating irrational numbers from the denominator of a fraction. For example, 1/√2 = √2/2. Laws of exponents for real numbers: a^m × a^n = a^(m+n), (a^m)^n = a^(mn), a^m / a^n = a^(m-n), a^0 = 1, and a^(-m) = 1/a^m.

Representation of irrational numbers on the number line can be done using geometric constructions (e.g., √2 using a right triangle with sides 1 and 1).

📐 √ab = √a × √b | √(a/b) = √a / √b

Chapter 2: Polynomials

A polynomial is an algebraic expression consisting of variables, coefficients, and non-negative integer exponents. The general form is p(x) = a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + ... + a_1 x + a_0, where a_n ≠ 0. The highest exponent n is the degree of the polynomial. Based on degree, polynomials are classified as linear (degree 1), quadratic (degree 2), cubic (degree 3), etc. A zero polynomial has all coefficients zero.

The value of a polynomial at x = a is p(a). A zero (or root) of a polynomial is a number c such that p(c) = 0. For a linear polynomial ax+b, the zero is -b/a. For a quadratic polynomial ax²+bx+c, the zeros can be found using the quadratic formula: x = [-b ± √(b²-4ac)] / 2a. The discriminant D = b²-4ac determines the nature of roots: D>0 (two distinct real roots), D=0 (one real repeated root), D<0 (no real roots).

The remainder theorem states that if a polynomial p(x) is divided by (x-a), the remainder is p(a). The factor theorem states that (x-a) is a factor of p(x) if and only if p(a)=0. This is used for factorisation.

Algebraic identities for polynomials: (x+y)² = x²+2xy+y², (x-y)² = x²-2xy+y², (x+y+z)² = x²+y²+z²+2xy+2yz+2zx, (x+y)³ = x³+y³+3xy(x+y), (x-y)³ = x³-y³-3xy(x-y), x³+y³ = (x+y)(x²-xy+y²), x³-y³ = (x-y)(x²+xy+y²).

📐 Remainder: p(a) | Factor: p(a)=0 ⇔ (x-a) is factor

Chapter 3: Coordinate Geometry

Coordinate geometry (also called analytic geometry) uses a coordinate system to represent geometric figures algebraically. The Cartesian plane is defined by two perpendicular number lines: the x-axis (horizontal) and the y-axis (vertical). Their intersection is the origin O(0,0). Any point P is represented by an ordered pair (x,y), where x is the x-coordinate (abscissa) and y is the y-coordinate (ordinate).

The plane is divided into four quadrants: Quadrant I (x>0, y>0), Quadrant II (x<0, y>0), Quadrant III (x<0, y<0), Quadrant IV (x>0, y<0). Points on the axes have either x=0 (y-axis) or y=0 (x-axis).

The distance between two points A(x₁,y₁) and B(x₂,y₂) is given by the distance formula: AB = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]. The section formula gives the coordinates of a point dividing a line segment in a given ratio. If a point divides AB in the ratio m:n internally, its coordinates are ((mx₂+nx₁)/(m+n), (my₂+ny₁)/(m+n)). The midpoint formula is a special case with m=n=1: ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2).

Area of a triangle with vertices (x₁,y₁), (x₂,y₂), (x₃,y₃) is ½ |x₁(y₂-y₃) + x₂(y₃-y₁) + x₃(y₁-y₂)|. If the area is zero, the three points are collinear.

📐 Distance = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²] | Midpoint = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)

Chapter 4: Linear Equations in Two Variables

A linear equation in two variables is of the form ax + by + c = 0, where a, b, c are real numbers, and a and b are not both zero. The graph of such an equation is a straight line. For example, 2x + 3y = 6 can be rewritten as y = (6-2x)/3. Every point (x,y) that satisfies the equation lies on the line, and every point on the line is a solution.

To graph a linear equation, we can find at least two solutions (x,y) by assigning arbitrary values to one variable and solving for the other. Plotting these points and joining them gives the line. The x-intercept is the point where the line crosses the x-axis (y=0). The y-intercept is where it crosses the y-axis (x=0).

Two lines can intersect at one point (unique solution), be parallel (no solution), or coincide (infinitely many solutions). The slope-intercept form is y = mx + c, where m is the slope and c is the y-intercept. The general form is Ax + By + C = 0.

Applications: Linear equations model real-life situations such as cost and revenue, speed and time, and mixture problems.

📐 y = mx + c | ax + by + c = 0

Chapter 5: Introduction to Euclid's Geometry

Euclid, the "father of geometry," wrote a book called "Elements" which laid the foundation for geometry. He introduced the axiomatic method: starting from definitions, axioms (self-evident truths), and postulates (assumptions specific to geometry), then proving theorems logically.

Euclid's definitions include: A point has no part; a line has length without breadth; a surface has length and breadth only; a straight line is the shortest distance between two points; a circle is a set of points equidistant from a center.

Euclid's axioms (common notions) are: Things equal to the same thing are equal; if equals are added to equals, the wholes are equal; if equals are subtracted from equals, the remainders are equal; things that coincide with one another are equal; the whole is greater than the part; things that are double of the same things are equal; things that are halves of the same things are equal.

Euclid's five postulates: 1) A straight line can be drawn from any point to any other point. 2) A finite straight line can be extended continuously. 3) A circle can be drawn with any center and radius. 4) All right angles are equal. 5) If a straight line falling on two straight lines makes interior angles on the same side less than two right angles, then the two lines, if extended indefinitely, meet on that side. This fifth postulate (parallel postulate) is equivalent to the statement: Through a point not on a line, exactly one line can be drawn parallel to the given line.

📐 Euclid's fifth postulate: Through a point not on a line, exactly one parallel line exists.

Chapter 6: Lines and Angles

Basic terms: A line segment has two endpoints. A ray has one endpoint and extends infinitely in one direction. An angle is formed when two rays originate from a common endpoint (vertex). Angles are classified as acute (<90°), right (=90°), obtuse (90°-180°), straight (=180°), reflex (>180°), and full (=360°). Complementary angles sum to 90°, supplementary angles sum to 180°.

When two lines intersect, vertically opposite angles are equal. Adjacent angles on a straight line are supplementary (linear pair axiom). If a transversal cuts two parallel lines, then: corresponding angles are equal, alternate interior angles are equal, and co-interior (consecutive interior) angles are supplementary. Conversely, if these conditions hold, the lines are parallel.

The angle sum property of a triangle: the sum of interior angles is 180°. An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two opposite interior angles.

📐 Sum of angles in triangle = 180° | Exterior angle = sum of remote interior angles

Chapter 7: Triangles

Triangles are classified by sides: equilateral (all sides equal), isosceles (two sides equal), scalene (all sides different). By angles: acute (all angles <90°), right (one angle 90°), obtuse (one angle >90°).

Congruence criteria: Two triangles are congruent if their corresponding sides and angles are equal. Criteria: SSS (side-side-side), SAS (side-angle-side), ASA (angle-side-angle), AAS (angle-angle-side), RHS (right angle-hypotenuse-side).

Inequalities in triangles: The sum of any two sides is greater than the third. The angle opposite the longer side is larger, and vice versa.

📐 SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, RHS congruence rules.

Chapter 8: Quadrilaterals

A quadrilateral has four sides, four vertices, and the sum of interior angles is 360°. Types: parallelogram (opposite sides parallel and equal), rectangle (parallelogram with right angles), square (all sides equal, all angles 90°), rhombus (all sides equal), trapezium (one pair of parallel sides), kite (two pairs of adjacent equal sides).

Properties: In a parallelogram, opposite sides and angles are equal, diagonals bisect each other. In a rectangle, diagonals are equal. In a rhombus, diagonals are perpendicular bisectors. In a square, all properties of rectangle and rhombus hold. The midpoint theorem: The line segment joining the midpoints of two sides of a triangle is parallel to the third side and half its length.

📐 Sum of interior angles = 360° | Midpoint theorem: segment joining midpoints is parallel and half.

Chapter 9: Circles

A circle is the set of all points equidistant from a fixed point (center). Radius is the distance from center to any point. Chord is a line segment joining two points on the circle. Diameter is the longest chord (passes through center). Arc is a part of the circumference. Sector is region between radii and arc. Segment is region between chord and arc.

Properties: Equal chords subtend equal angles at the center. The perpendicular from the center to a chord bisects the chord. The angle subtended by an arc at the center is twice the angle at the circumference. Angles in the same segment are equal. The angle in a semicircle is a right angle (90°). Cyclic quadrilaterals: sum of opposite angles = 180°.

📐 Angle at center = 2 × angle at circumference | Opposite angles of cyclic quadrilateral = 180°

Chapter 10: Heron's Formula

Heron's formula calculates the area of a triangle when all three sides are known, without needing height. For a triangle with sides a, b, c, semi-perimeter s = (a+b+c)/2. Then area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)].

This formula is useful for scalene triangles and for finding areas of quadrilaterals by dividing them into triangles.

📐 s = (a+b+c)/2 | Area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]

Chapter 11: Surface Areas and Volumes

Formulas for solids:

  • Cuboid: LSA = 2h(l+b), TSA = 2(lb+bh+hl), Volume = l×b×h
  • Cube: LSA = 4a², TSA = 6a², Volume = a³
  • Cylinder: CSA = 2πrh, TSA = 2πr(r+h), Volume = πr²h
  • Cone: CSA = πrl, TSA = πr(r+l), Volume = (1/3)πr²h, where l = √(r²+h²)
  • Sphere: SA = 4πr², Volume = (4/3)πr³
  • Hemisphere: CSA = 2πr², TSA = 3πr², Volume = (2/3)πr³
📐 Volume of cylinder = πr²h | Volume of cone = ⅓πr²h | Volume of sphere = ⁴⁄₃πr³

Chapter 12: Statistics

Statistics deals with collection, presentation, analysis, and interpretation of data. Data can be raw or grouped. Measures of central tendency: mean (average), median (middle value), mode (most frequent). For ungrouped data, mean = sum of all observations / number of observations. Median: arrange data ascending; if n odd, median = middle; if even, median = average of two middle values. Mode = value that occurs most often.

Graphical representation: bar graphs, histograms (for grouped continuous data), frequency polygons.

📐 Mean = Σx/n | Median = middle value | Mode = most frequent

Chapter 13: Probability

Probability measures the likelihood of an event. For an experiment with equally likely outcomes, probability of event E = Number of favourable outcomes / Total number of outcomes. Probability ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

Examples: Tossing a coin: P(head)=1/2, P(tail)=1/2. Rolling a die: P(6)=1/6, P(even)=3/6=1/2. Drawing a card from a deck: P(ace)=4/52=1/13.

The sum of probabilities of all elementary events is 1. Complementary event: P(not E)=1-P(E).

📐 P(E) = favourable/total | 0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1
