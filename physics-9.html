Chapter 1: Motion

1.1 What is Motion?
Motion is the change in position of an object with respect to time and a reference point. In physics, an object is said to be in motion if its position changes relative to a fixed point (origin). For example, a car moving on a road, a flying bird, or the Earth revolving around the Sun are all in motion. An object is at rest if it does not change its position with respect to its surroundings, like a book lying on a table. Motion is relative; the same object may be at rest relative to one observer but in motion relative to another. For instance, a passenger sitting in a moving train is at rest relative to the train but in motion relative to the ground.

1.2 Types of Motion
Based on the path, motion is classified as: Rectilinear motion (straight line path, e.g., a car on a straight road), Curvilinear motion (curved path, e.g., a thrown ball), Circular motion (path is a circle, e.g., a satellite), Rotational motion (spinning about an axis, e.g., a spinning top), and Oscillatory/Periodic motion (to and fro motion, e.g., a pendulum).

1.3 Distance and Displacement
Distance is the total path length traveled by an object, irrespective of direction. It is a scalar quantity (only magnitude). SI unit: metre (m). Displacement is the shortest straight-line distance from the initial to the final position, along with direction. It is a vector quantity. For a body returning to its starting point, displacement is zero while distance is positive. Example: If you walk 4 m east then 3 m north, distance = 7 m, but displacement = √(4²+3²) = 5 m at an angle.

1.4 Speed and Velocity
Speed = Distance / Time. It is scalar. Average speed = total distance / total time. Instantaneous speed is speed at a given instant. Velocity = Displacement / Time. It is vector. Uniform velocity means equal displacement in equal time intervals; non-uniform means unequal. Example: A car moving at constant 60 km/h on a straight road has uniform velocity; if it turns, velocity changes even if speed constant.

1.5 Acceleration
Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. a = (v-u)/t, where u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, t = time. SI unit: m/s². Positive acceleration means velocity increases; negative acceleration (deceleration/retardation) means velocity decreases. Uniform acceleration means constant rate (e.g., free fall under gravity, a = 9.8 m/s²). Non-uniform acceleration occurs when rate changes.

1.6 Equations of Motion (Uniform Acceleration)
For motion in a straight line with uniform acceleration:
(i) v = u + at
(ii) s = ut + ½ at²
(iii) v² = u² + 2as
These are derived from the definitions. Example: A car starts from rest (u=0) and accelerates at 2 m/s² for 5 s. v = 0+2×5 = 10 m/s, s = 0×5 + ½×2×25 = 25 m.

1.7 Graphical Representation of Motion
Distance-Time graph: For uniform motion, it is a straight line (slope = speed). For non-uniform motion, it is curved. Velocity-Time graph: For uniform acceleration, it is a straight line with slope = acceleration. Area under v-t graph gives displacement. Example: For constant velocity, v-t graph is horizontal line; area = velocity × time = displacement.

1.8 Uniform Circular Motion
When an object moves on a circular path with constant speed, it is called uniform circular motion. The direction changes continuously, so velocity changes, hence acceleration exists (centripetal acceleration, directed towards center). Formula: a_c = v²/r, where v = speed, r = radius. Example: A stone whirled in a circle, a satellite orbiting Earth.

1.9 Numerical Examples
1. A train accelerates from rest to 20 m/s in 10 s. Find acceleration. a = (20-0)/10 = 2 m/s².
2. A car moving at 30 m/s applies brakes and stops in 6 s. Find deceleration. a = (0-30)/6 = -5 m/s².

📐 v = u + at | s = ut + ½ at² | v² = u² + 2as

Chapter 2: Force and Laws of Motion

2.1 Force – Definition and Effects
A force is a push or pull that can change the state of rest or motion of an object. It can also change the shape or size of an object. SI unit: Newton (N). Force is a vector (magnitude + direction). Effects: (i) move a stationary object, (ii) stop a moving object, (iii) change speed or direction, (iv) change shape. Example: Kicking a football moves it; brakes stop a car; hitting a ball changes direction; squeezing a sponge changes shape.

2.2 Balanced and Unbalanced Forces
When multiple forces act on an object, their net effect is the resultant force. If the resultant is zero, forces are balanced; they do not change motion (object remains at rest or moves with constant velocity). If resultant is non-zero, forces are unbalanced; they cause acceleration. Example: A book on a table – gravity pulls down, table pushes up (balanced). Pushing a box with 10 N while friction is 8 N – net force 2 N (unbalanced) – box accelerates.

2.3 Newton's First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)
Statement: An object remains at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external unbalanced force. This property of objects to resist change in state is called inertia. Mass is a measure of inertia; heavier objects have more inertia. Examples: Passengers fall backward when a bus starts suddenly (inertia of rest). Passengers fall forward when bus stops suddenly (inertia of motion). Dust particles fall off a carpet when beaten – inertia of rest.

2.4 Newton's Second Law of Motion
Statement: The rate of change of momentum of an object is directly proportional to the applied unbalanced force and takes place in the direction of the force. Momentum p = m × v. Rate of change of momentum = (mv - mu)/t = m(v-u)/t = m a. Thus F = k m a. In SI units, k=1, so F = m a. One Newton is the force that accelerates a 1 kg mass at 1 m/s². Impulse = F × t = change in momentum. Example: A cricket player pulls his hands back while catching a ball – increases time, reduces force (impulse constant).

2.5 Newton's Third Law of Motion
Statement: To every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Action and reaction act on different bodies, never cancel each other. Examples: A rocket pushes exhaust gases downward (action), gases push rocket upward (reaction). While walking, foot pushes ground backward (action), ground pushes foot forward (reaction). A book on table – book exerts force on table (action), table exerts equal upward force on book (reaction).

2.6 Conservation of Momentum
When two or more objects interact, the total momentum of the system remains constant if no external force acts. Mathematically, m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂. This is derived from Newton's third law. Example: A gun recoils when fired – momentum of bullet forward equals momentum of gun backward.

2.7 Numerical Examples
1. A force of 10 N acts on a 2 kg mass. Find acceleration. a = F/m = 10/2 = 5 m/s².
2. A 0.5 kg ball moving at 10 m/s is stopped in 0.1 s. Find force. Impulse = change in momentum = 0.5×10 = 5 Ns. F = Impulse/t = 5/0.1 = 50 N.

📐 F = ma | Momentum p = mv | Impulse = FΔt = Δp

Chapter 3: Gravitation

3.1 Universal Law of Gravitation
Every object in the universe attracts every other object with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers. F = G × (m₁ m₂)/r², where G = 6.67×10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg² (universal gravitational constant). This law explains the motion of planets, the moon, and the force that binds us to Earth.

3.2 Importance of Gravitation
Gravitation is responsible for: (i) the Earth's gravity pulling objects downward, (ii) the Moon revolving around Earth, (iii) planets revolving around the Sun, (iv) tides due to Moon's pull, (v) the structure of the universe.

3.3 Acceleration due to Gravity (g)
When an object falls freely under gravity, its acceleration is g. For Earth, g = GM/R², where M = Earth's mass (6×10²⁴ kg), R = Earth's radius (6.4×10⁶ m). Approx g = 9.8 m/s². Value of g varies with altitude, depth, and latitude. At poles, g is maximum; at equator, minimum. g decreases with height: g' = g(1 - 2h/R) for small h.

3.4 Mass and Weight
Mass is the amount of matter in a body; constant everywhere. Weight = mass × g; varies with location. Weight on Moon = (1/6) weight on Earth because Moon's mass is smaller. Example: A 60 kg person has weight 588 N on Earth, but only 98 N on Moon.

3.5 Free Fall
Free fall is motion under only gravity (no air resistance). Equations of motion apply with a = g. For an object dropped from height h: v = √(2gh), t = √(2h/g).

3.6 Thrust and Pressure
Thrust is force acting perpendicularly on a surface. Pressure = Thrust / Area. SI unit: Pascal (Pa). Pressure in fluids increases with depth. Buoyancy: upward force exerted by a fluid. Archimedes' principle: Buoyant force = weight of displaced fluid.

3.7 Numerical Examples
1. Find gravitational force between two 100 kg objects separated by 1 m. F = (6.67e-11 × 100×100)/1² = 6.67×10⁻⁷ N.
2. A 10 kg object weighs 98 N on Earth. What is its weight on Moon? Weight = (10×9.8)/6 = 16.33 N.

📐 F = Gm₁m₂/r² | g = GM/R² | Weight = mg

Chapter 4: Work and Energy

4.1 Work – Definition and Conditions
Work is done when a force causes a displacement. W = F × d × cosθ, where θ is angle between force and displacement. Work is scalar, SI unit joule (J). Conditions: force must act, displacement must occur, and force must have component along displacement. Work is positive if 0≤θ<90°, negative if 90°<θ≤180° (e.g., friction), zero if θ=90° (e.g., carrying a bag horizontally while walking – force upward, displacement horizontal).

4.2 Energy – Capacity to do Work
Energy is the ability to do work. Same unit as work (joule). Forms: kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical, electrical, nuclear, etc. Law of conservation of energy: energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. Total energy of an isolated system remains constant.

4.3 Kinetic Energy (KE)
Energy possessed by a body due to its motion. KE = ½ mv². Derivation: Work done by force F to accelerate from rest to v over displacement s: W = F s = ma s, and v²=2as => s=v²/(2a), so W = m a × v²/(2a)=½ mv². Thus KE = ½ mv². Example: A 1000 kg car at 20 m/s has KE = 0.5×1000×400 = 200,000 J.

4.4 Potential Energy (PE)
Energy stored due to position or configuration. Gravitational PE = mgh, where h is height above reference. Elastic PE in a spring = ½ kx². Example: A 2 kg object at 5 m height has PE = 2×9.8×5 = 98 J.

4.5 Conservation of Mechanical Energy
In the absence of non-conservative forces (like friction), total mechanical energy (KE+PE) is constant. For a falling object: at height h, PE = mgh, KE = 0; just before ground, KE = ½ mv² = mgh, PE = 0. So v = √(2gh).

4.6 Power
Power is the rate of doing work. P = W/t. SI unit: watt (W), 1 W = 1 J/s. Commercial unit: kilowatt-hour (kWh) = 3.6×10⁶ J. Example: A 100 W bulb consumes 0.1 kWh in 1 hour.

4.7 Numerical Examples
1. A 50 N force moves an object 10 m at 30° to force. Work = 50×10×cos30° = 500×0.866 = 433 J.
2. A 10 kg object falls from 20 m. Find KE just before ground. KE = mgh = 10×9.8×20 = 1960 J.

📐 W = Fd cosθ | KE = ½ mv² | PE = mgh | P = W/t

Chapter 5: Sound

5.1 Production and Propagation of Sound
Sound is produced by vibrating objects. It requires a medium (solid, liquid, gas) to travel; cannot travel in vacuum. Sound waves are mechanical longitudinal waves, consisting of compressions (high pressure) and rarefactions (low pressure). The speed of sound depends on medium: solids > liquids > gases. In air at 20°C, v ≈ 343 m/s. Speed increases with temperature.

5.2 Characteristics of Sound Wave
Amplitude (A): maximum displacement from mean position; determines loudness (larger amplitude → louder). Frequency (f): number of oscillations per second (Hz); determines pitch (higher f → higher pitch). Wavelength (λ): distance between two consecutive compressions or rarefactions. Relationship: v = f λ. Time period (T) = 1/f.

5.3 Audible Range and Applications
Humans hear 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Below 20 Hz: infrasound (elephants, earthquakes). Above 20 kHz: ultrasound (medical imaging, sonar, cleaning).

5.4 Reflection of Sound – Echo and Reverberation
Sound reflects from surfaces. An echo is heard when reflected sound arrives after 0.1 s; minimum distance = v×t/2 = 343×0.1/2 ≈ 17 m. Reverberation is persistence of sound due to multiple reflections; reduced by sound-absorbing materials in auditoriums.

5.5 Human Ear
Outer ear (pinna) collects sound → ear canal → eardrum vibrates → middle ear (hammer, anvil, stirrup) amplifies → inner ear (cochlea) converts to electrical signals → brain.

5.6 Numerical Examples
1. A sound wave has frequency 500 Hz and wavelength 0.68 m. Find speed. v = fλ = 500×0.68 = 340 m/s.
2. If you hear an echo after 0.2 s from a cliff, distance = 343×0.2/2 = 34.3 m.

📐 v = fλ | Echo distance = v×t/2 | Audible 20 Hz – 20 kHz
