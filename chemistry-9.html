Chapter 1: Matter in Our Surroundings

1.1 What is Matter?
Matter is anything that occupies space and has mass. All living and non-living things are made of matter. Examples: air, water, food, plants, animals, rocks. Matter is composed of tiny particles called atoms and molecules. These particles are in constant random motion and have spaces between them.

1.2 Physical Nature of Matter
Particles of matter are very small – they cannot be seen with the naked eye. The particles attract each other (force of attraction). The strength of attraction depends on the state of matter: strongest in solids, weaker in liquids, weakest in gases.

1.3 States of Matter
Solid: Fixed shape and volume, particles tightly packed, only vibrate. Liquid: Fixed volume but takes shape of container, particles can slide past each other. Gas: Neither fixed shape nor volume, particles move freely and fill any container. Plasma and Bose-Einstein condensate are other states but not in Class 9 syllabus.

1.4 Diffusion – Evidence of Particle Motion
Diffusion is the intermixing of particles of different substances due to their random motion. It is fastest in gases, slower in liquids, and extremely slow in solids. Example: The smell of perfume spreads throughout a room – gas particles diffuse. A drop of ink spreads in water – liquid diffusion.

1.5 Brownian Motion
The random zigzag movement of tiny particles suspended in a fluid is called Brownian motion. It provides direct evidence of the continuous random motion of particles.

1.6 Change of State
Matter can change from one state to another by altering temperature or pressure. Melting (fusion): solid → liquid at melting point. Freezing: liquid → solid. Vaporization (boiling): liquid → gas at boiling point. Evaporation: liquid → gas below boiling point (surface phenomenon). Condensation: gas → liquid. Sublimation: solid directly to gas (e.g., camphor, dry ice, iodine). Deposition: gas directly to solid.

1.7 Latent Heat
The heat energy required to change the state of a substance without changing its temperature is called latent heat. Latent heat of fusion: heat to convert 1 kg solid to liquid at melting point (for ice: 334 kJ/kg). Latent heat of vaporization: heat to convert 1 kg liquid to gas at boiling point (for water: 2260 kJ/kg).

1.8 Evaporation and Cooling
Evaporation causes cooling because the fastest-moving (highest-energy) particles escape from the liquid surface, leaving behind lower-energy particles. Factors affecting evaporation: (i) temperature – higher temperature increases rate, (ii) surface area – larger area increases rate, (iii) humidity – lower humidity increases rate, (iv) wind speed – higher wind increases rate.

1.9 Effect of Pressure on State
Increasing pressure can liquefy gases (e.g., LPG – butane stored under pressure). Decreasing pressure can cause boiling at lower temperatures.

1.10 Numerical Examples
1. Calculate the energy required to melt 2 kg of ice at 0°C. Energy = m × L_f = 2 × 334 = 668 kJ.
2. How much energy is released when 0.5 kg of steam condenses at 100°C? Q = 0.5 × 2260 = 1130 kJ.

📐 Latent heat of fusion = 334 kJ/kg | Latent heat of vaporization = 2260 kJ/kg

Chapter 2: Is Matter Around Us Pure?

2.1 Pure Substances and Mixtures
A pure substance consists of a single type of particle (element or compound). Examples: gold, water (H₂O), oxygen. A mixture contains two or more pure substances physically combined. Mixtures can be homogeneous (uniform composition, e.g., salt solution) or heterogeneous (non-uniform, e.g., sand and water).

2.2 Solutions
A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. Solute – substance that dissolves. Solvent – dissolving medium. A saturated solution cannot dissolve more solute at a given temperature; an unsaturated solution can. Concentration can be expressed as mass by mass percentage = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100, or mass by volume percentage.

2.3 Suspensions and Colloids
A suspension is a heterogeneous mixture where particles are large (>1000 nm) and settle down on standing. Example: muddy water. A colloid has particle size between 1 nm and 1000 nm. Colloids show the Tyndall effect – scattering of light by colloidal particles. Examples: milk, fog, smoke, jelly.

2.4 Separation Techniques
Various methods are used to separate mixtures:
Filtration: separates insoluble solids from liquids.
Evaporation: separates a soluble solid from a liquid.
Centrifugation: separates based on density (e.g., cream from milk).
Distillation: separates liquids with different boiling points.
Fractional distillation: separates mixtures of miscible liquids with close boiling points (e.g., crude oil).
Chromatography: separates components based on their affinity to a stationary phase (e.g., ink pigments).
Crystallization: obtains pure crystals from a solution.

2.5 Elements and Compounds
An element is a pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. Examples: iron, oxygen, gold. Elements are classified as metals, non-metals, and metalloids. A compound is formed when two or more elements chemically combine in a fixed ratio. Compounds have properties different from their constituent elements. Example: water (H₂O) is a liquid, but hydrogen and oxygen are gases.

2.6 Numerical Examples
1. Calculate the mass percentage of sugar in a solution containing 15 g sugar dissolved in 85 g water. Total mass = 100 g, percentage = (15/100)×100 = 15%.
2. How much salt is needed to prepare 200 g of a 10% salt solution? Mass of salt = (10/100)×200 = 20 g.

📐 Mass% = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100

Chapter 3: Atoms and Molecules

3.1 Dalton's Atomic Theory
John Dalton proposed that matter is made of indivisible particles called atoms. Key postulates: (i) All matter consists of atoms. (ii) Atoms of the same element are identical in mass and properties. (iii) Atoms of different elements have different masses. (iv) Compounds are formed by the combination of atoms in fixed ratios. (v) Chemical reactions involve rearrangement of atoms – atoms are neither created nor destroyed (law of conservation of mass).

3.2 Laws of Chemical Combination
Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier): Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O; total mass of reactants equals total mass of products.
Law of Constant Proportions (Proust): A chemical compound always contains the same elements in the same fixed proportion by mass. Example: Water always has H:O mass ratio = 1:8, regardless of source.

3.3 Atoms – The Building Blocks
An atom is the smallest particle of an element that retains its chemical properties. Atoms of most elements do not exist freely; they combine to form molecules. Atomic mass is the relative mass of an atom compared to 1/12th the mass of a carbon-12 atom. The modern atomic mass unit (u) is defined as 1/12th of the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

3.4 Molecules and Ions
A molecule is the smallest particle of a compound or element that can exist independently. Diatomic molecules: H₂, O₂, N₂, Cl₂. Polyatomic molecules: O₃, P₄, S₈. Ions are charged particles: cations (positive) and anions (negative). Example: Na⁺, Cl⁻. Ionic compounds are formed by the transfer of electrons.

3.5 Molecular Mass and Formula Unit Mass
Molecular mass is the sum of atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule. Example: H₂O = 2×1 + 16 = 18 u. For ionic compounds like NaCl, we use formula unit mass = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 u.

3.6 The Mole Concept
One mole of any substance contains the same number of particles – Avogadro's number, 6.022 × 10²³. The mass of one mole of a substance is its molar mass (numerically equal to atomic/molecular mass in grams). Number of moles = given mass / molar mass. Number of particles = number of moles × Avogadro's number. Example: 18 g of water = 1 mole = 6.022×10²³ molecules.

3.7 Numerical Examples
1. Find the number of moles in 44 g of CO₂. Molar mass CO₂ = 12+32=44 g/mol. Moles = 44/44 = 1 mole.
2. How many molecules are in 90 g of water? Molar mass H₂O=18 g/mol. Moles=90/18=5. Molecules = 5 × 6.022×10²³ = 3.011×10²⁴.

📐 1 mole = 6.022×10²³ particles | Moles = mass / molar mass

Chapter 4: Structure of the Atom

4.1 Discovery of Subatomic Particles
Electron (e⁻): Discovered by J.J. Thomson using cathode ray tubes. Electrons are negatively charged with charge = -1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C and mass = 9.1×10⁻³¹ kg.
Proton (p⁺): Discovered by E. Goldstein using anode rays. Positive charge = +1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C, mass ≈ 1836 times electron.
Neutron (n⁰): Discovered by James Chadwick. Neutral particle with mass slightly larger than proton.

4.2 Thomson's Model (Plum Pudding)
Thomson proposed that an atom is a sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded in it (like raisins in a pudding). This model was later disproved by Rutherford.

4.3 Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment
Rutherford bombarded a thin gold foil with alpha (α) particles. Observations: (i) Most α-particles passed straight through. (ii) Some were deflected at small angles. (iii) Very few (1 in 20,000) bounced back. Conclusions: (a) Atom has a small, dense, positively charged nucleus. (b) Most of the atom is empty space. (c) Electrons revolve around the nucleus. Drawback: Could not explain stability of atoms – electrons would spiral into the nucleus.

4.4 Bohr's Model
Niels Bohr improved Rutherford's model: (i) Electrons revolve in fixed energy levels (shells) called K, L, M, N... (ii) They do not radiate energy while in these orbits. (iii) Energy is emitted or absorbed only when an electron jumps from one orbit to another. Maximum number of electrons in a shell = 2n² (n=1,2,3...). K=2, L=8, M=18, N=32. Outermost shell cannot have more than 8 electrons (octet rule).

4.5 Atomic Number and Mass Number
Atomic number (Z) = number of protons = number of electrons in a neutral atom. Mass number (A) = number of protons + neutrons. Neutrons = A - Z. Example: Carbon-12 has Z=6, A=12, neutrons=6.

4.6 Isotopes and Isobars
Isotopes: Same atomic number, different mass number (different neutrons). Example: Hydrogen: ¹H (protium), ²H (deuterium), ³H (tritium). Isotopes have same chemical properties but different physical properties.
Isobars: Same mass number, different atomic number. Example: Argon-40 (Z=18) and Calcium-40 (Z=20).

4.7 Electronic Configuration and Valency
Electrons are distributed in shells according to the Bohr-Bury scheme. Valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost shell. Valency is the combining capacity: for metals, valency = number of valence electrons; for non-metals, valency = 8 - valence electrons. Example: Sodium (Na, Z=11) has configuration 2,8,1 → valency = 1. Chlorine (Cl, Z=17) has 2,8,7 → valency = 1.

4.8 Numerical Examples
1. Find the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in an atom of chlorine-35 (Z=17, A=35). Protons=17, electrons=17, neutrons=35-17=18.
2. Write the electronic configuration of oxygen (Z=8). Configuration: 2,6.

📐 Z = p⁺ = e⁻ | A = p⁺ + n⁰ | Valency = 8 - valence e⁻ (for non-metals)
